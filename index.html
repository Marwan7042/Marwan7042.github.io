Triton ROVs is a 40-member student team at AASTMT that competes in the MATE ROV competition — one of the most demanding student robotics competitions globally. The team covers mechanical, electrical, and software engineering, and has to integrate all three under competition pressure.

As part of the Vision & Autonomy subteam, I worked on the perception and streaming systems. On the perception side, I built a full Visual Inertial Odometry pipeline using an Error-State Kalman Filter (ESKF) with hand-derived Jacobians, since GPS doesn't work underwater. I also deployed YOLOv8 on an Intel MyriadX VPU via OpenVINO, which required debugging CMX memory overflow issues and identifying an architectural incompatibility between YOLOv11 attention blocks and the MyriadX.

On the streaming side, I built a lock-free multi-camera system that handles 5 simultaneous feeds on a Raspberry Pi 4, keeping CPU below 70% through Broadcom ISP offloading and core affinity pinning. The network stack uses a dual-protocol design — zero-copy binary video over WebSockets isolated from REST control polling — to avoid blocking under concurrent load.

The team won 1st Place Regionally in Egypt and 3rd Place at the World Championship in Canada. The team also received the Best Engineering Presentation and Best Technical Documentation awards — a collective effort across all 40 members.
