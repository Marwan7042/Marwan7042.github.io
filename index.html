Chapter 01 · The Crucible

We built a robot. Then we took it to Canada.

GPS doesn't work underwater. Cameras see nothing in murky depths. The competition clock is always ticking. These are not hypothetical engineering constraints — they were ours to solve, for real, in 2026, under international judges' eyes.

"Every line of code I wrote had to work under 30 meters of water, on hardware the size of a shoebox, in real time."

As the Vision & Autonomy software lead for Triton ROVs, I built the entire perception and autonomy stack: a full VIO pipeline using an Error-State Kalman Filter with hand-derived Jacobians, stereo depth measurement with ±cm precision, and a 5-camera live streaming system that kept CPU usage below 70% on a Raspberry Pi 4.

We won 1st Place Regionally and 3rd Place Internationally. Best Engineering Presentation. Best Technical Documentation. And a bronze medal 12,000 km from home.

Chapter 02 · The Machine

I didn't use PyTorch. I built it.

Most people learn deep learning by importing libraries. I wanted to understand exactly what happens when a model trains — not just conceptually, but at the level of raw memory, mathematics, and machine instructions.

So I started at zero. I wrote my own STL containers (vectors, hash maps, linked lists) to manage memory. On top of that, I built a Tensor class with N-dimensional strides and zero-copy transpose. Then, the Autograd engine: a reverse-mode topological sort that tracks every mathematical operation and computes exact gradients.

"When I finally watched the loss curve drop to 0.22 on the RTX 5050 — knowing I had written every single operation from scratch — it felt like building a fire with stones."

The final piece was integrating NVIDIA CUDA and cuBLAS, offloading matrix multiplications across the PCIe bus to the GPU. The result: 95.2% accuracy on MNIST, trained by a framework I wrote entirely in C++.
